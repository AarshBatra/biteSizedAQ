Air quality is usually discussed using annual averages, city rankings,

## What the compliance stripes show

Each panel represents one Indian state or union territory.

- Columns: Years (1998‚Äì2024)

- Rows: Months (January to December)

- Colour: Percentage of subdistricts that are compliant

Compliance is always defined explicitly ‚Äî either:

- üåç with respect to the WHO PM‚ÇÇ.‚ÇÖ guideline

Each coloured cell answers:

> In this state, in this month, in this year ‚Äî what fraction of

The structure is identical. Only the benchmark changes.

## National standard view: a strongly seasonal problem

When compliance is measured with respect to the national PM‚ÇÇ.‚ÇÖ standard,
a powerful and familiar pattern appears.

Across almost all states:

- July‚ÄìSeptember (monsoon months) show high compliance

- November‚ÄìFebruary (winter months) show widespread non-compliance

Visually, this appears as:

- Horizontal green bands in the monsoon

- Horizontal red bands in winter

Averaged across all states and all years:

- Monsoon months frequently show 60‚Äì100% subdistrict compliance

- January‚ÄìFebruary drop sharply, often below 40% compliance

## What this tells us

Under the national standard:

- Meteorology matters enormously

- Rain, higher boundary layers, and dispersion repeatedly push
  concentrations below 40 ¬µg/m¬≥

- Compliance appears to ‚Äúrecover‚Äù every year

From this perspective, India‚Äôs air-quality problem looks:

**Severe, seasonal, and partially reversible**

## National standard view also preserves spatial differences

Measured with respect to the national standard, states remain clearly
distinguishable.

### Mostly compliant states

Kerala, Arunachal Pradesh, Andaman & Nicobar Islands, Jammu & Kashmir,
Karnataka

### Persistently non-compliant states

- Delhi‚Äôs average compliance is extremely low

This view supports a familiar narrative:

## WHO guideline view: the seasonality collapses

Now look at the same stripes, but measure compliance with respect to the
WHO PM‚ÇÇ.‚ÇÖ guideline.

The visual changes completely.

- Reds dominate almost every state

- Greens become rare, fragile, and localized

- Even historically ‚Äúclean‚Äù states turn predominantly red

Under the WHO guideline:

- Kerala and Karnataka no longer appear clean

- Himalayan and coastal advantages weaken dramatically

- Only a few places (e.g.¬†Lakshadweep, parts of the Northeast, Andaman
  and Nicobar Islands) show limited relief

## The most important change: seasonality largely disappears

This is the central insight.

Under the WHO guideline:

- Monsoon months no longer bring widespread compliance

- Winter is bad ‚Äî but summer is also bad

- The horizontal green bands vanish

In other words:

> **The air does not become health-safe in any season for most of
> India.**

The strong seasonality seen under the national standard is not a
property of air quality alone ‚Äî it is a property of the threshold.

## What this contrast really means

The national standard and WHO guideline are applied to the same
pollution data.

Yet:

- One produces a story of seasonal crisis and partial recovery

- The other produces a story of persistent, year-round non-compliance

This tells us something fundamental:

> **Seasonality in compliance is largely created by where the bar is
> set.**

The national standard sits above large parts of India‚Äôs seasonal PM‚ÇÇ.‚ÇÖ
distribution.  
The WHO guideline sits below it.

As a result:

- Monsoon months cross the national threshold

- They do not cross the WHO threshold

## How spatial differences also flatten under WHO compliance

Under the national standard:

- Delhi looks far worse than Kerala

- Punjab worse than Tamil Nadu

- Haryana worse than Odisha

Under the WHO guideline:

- Almost all states look bad

- Differences compress

- Non-compliance becomes systemic rather than regional

This does not mean pollution levels are identical everywhere. It means
that almost everywhere exceeds a health-based limit.

## What this visual does not show (by design)

These stripes represent:

- Percentage of subdistricts, not people

- Compliance coverage, not exposure or health burden

A state can appear moderately compliant while:

- A few highly polluted urban subdistricts house millions

This visual is not a burden estimate. It is a coverage diagnostic.

It answers:

> How widespread is clean air ‚Äî under a given definition of ‚Äúclean‚Äù?

## The core takeaway

India‚Äôs air quality problem only looks seasonal because the national
standard is permissive.

Measured against a health-based benchmark:

- Non-compliance is persistent

- Seasonal relief largely disappears

- Clean air is the exception, not the norm

Both conclusions come from the same data. The difference lies entirely
in the standard chosen.

## Why the problem still doesn‚Äôt resolve: understanding airsheds

At this point, the data has already told us something stark:

- The problem is **chronic**, not episodic

- It persists **across seasons**

- It affects **nearly all states** under a health-based benchmark

The natural next question is: **why does this persist, even when some
states try to act?**

The answer lies in something we rarely design policy around:
**airsheds**.

An *airshed* is the atmospheric equivalent of a river basin. Just as
water flows downhill across districts and states, **air moves across
large, connected regions shaped by geography, wind patterns, and
topography**.

A river basin is visible but an airshed isn‚Äôt, but this intangibleness
of the airsheds leads to a natural ignorance of its importance. This
ignorance is very dangerous. Having an airshed based approach to tackle
air pollution is non-negotiable if we want to make a dent in this
problem.

Within an airshed:

- Pollution emitted in one place does not stay local

- Emissions mix, accumulate, and travel

- Downwind regions inherit upwind decisions

State borders do not interrupt this flow. Mountains, plains, coastlines,
and prevailing winds do.

## Why airsheds help paint a more comprehensive story

The WHO comparison tells us **how bad the problem is**. Airsheds explain
**why it refuses to go away**.

Large parts of India are not failing independently. They are failing as
**connected atmospheric systems**.

Local, city-level, or even state-level actions operate *inside* these
systems. Their impact is therefore limited unless the entire airshed
changes.

This is why:

- Improvements plateau

- Winter action plans struggle

- Compliance rarely shifts under health-based benchmarks

## Conclusion: the mismatch at the heart of India‚Äôs air crisis

Put all the pieces together:

- **The national standard makes the problem look seasonal**

- **The WHO guideline reveals it as chronic**

- **Airsheds show why isolated fixes cannot succeed**

India‚Äôs air quality crisis is not:

- Just a winter problem

- Just a Delhi problem

- Just a state-wise problem

It is a **persistent, transboundary, atmospheric problem** being managed
with tools designed for administrative convenience, not physical
reality.

As long as we:

- Judge success using permissive thresholds

- Design policy within political borders

- Treat pollution as episodic rather than structural

We will keep mistaking movement for progress.

Clean air will appear and disappear on charts ‚Äî while exposure remains.

The data is already telling us what to do.

We need to stop managing maps, and start managing the atmosphere.
